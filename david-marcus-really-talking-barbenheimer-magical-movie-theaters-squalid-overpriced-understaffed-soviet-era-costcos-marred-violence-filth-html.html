DAVID MARCUS: What everyone is REALLY talking about after Barbenheimer... How our once magical movie

July 02, 2024
blog

For the hordes of Americans rushing back to the movie theaters this weekend for the rare pleasure of a summer blockbuster a sinking realization awaits.

The magic of the movie house is gone, and likely, for good.

Barbieheimer – the dueling release of two potential Hollywood hits, Barbie and Oppenheimer – may have given us a false sense of hope.

But anyone who's recently settled into a gummy, upholstered seat to watch the latest Tinseltown flick knows what I'm talking about.

The enchanting silver screens of our childhoods have been transformed into dens of dingy darkness - and even danger.

Video emerged this week of a shocking attack on a 63-year-old man in Florida, who had the temerity to ask a couple to move from the preassigned seat that he had purchased.

The younger aggressor launched himself at the man, knocking him to the ground, before pummelling him in the face, as the trailer for the new Mission Impossible movie played in the background.

Sure, that can happen anywhere, but it says so much about going to the movies today.

It's about as fun as being punched in the face.

Movie theater companies are in a death spiral.

When we all went into COVID lockdowns studios reduced their output, their inventories went direct to streaming, box office sales fell off a cliff and theaters shut their doors in droves.

Between 2019 and 2022, 2,165 U.S. cinemas – more than 5 percent of the screens in the country - closed for good.

Many of the ones that survived are left limping along. Ticket prices are up (the national average is $10.53 from $9.16 in 2019) and the quality of the experience is way down.

It's not just the threat of physical violence that mars the post-pandemic excursion to the cinema.

The whole experience is a dank, depressing metaphor for a society that barely knows how to function in public anymore.

Walking into your local theater, once a buzz with video game sounds, wild kids, coy couples on charming first dates, and an air of excitement, feels like trudging into some kind of Soviet-era Costco.

At the concession stands, when they are open, one can wait in lines for hours for the privilege of spending the approximate price of fine dining in Paris for a bucket of stale popcorn or hot dogs of a strange greenish hue.

Shelves – once packed with a wide variety of candy and treats – are empty.

With a second mortgage worth of inedible snacks in your hands, you fumble on your phone to find your exact seat, which was purchased online, of course. God forbid, we have too much human interaction.

Gone are the meritocratic days of first-come, first-served. Once, if you had it together, you arrived at the theater early and earned a prime spot in the middle of the audience.

Now the rich and frivolous rule. Those willing to shell out extra for a 'preferred' seat can roll in at the tail end of the coming attractions to step and stumble over patrons who had the consideration to arrive on time.

And those who refused to pay a premium end up in the first row craning their necks at a 90-degree angle.

If you're lucky enough to get to your seat without getting assaulted, the lights sometimes go fully down, sometimes not, sometimes it's cold, sometimes hot.

The sticky floors and musty smells seem to whisper, 'we are understaffed, just deal with it.'

The sloppy kid who took your ticket, also worked the concession counter, swept the floors and restocked the men's room.

The theaters clearly can't afford to hire a sufficient number of workers or pay their employees enough to make them care.

When there is staff to be seen and dealt with, they are of a Gen Z variety, who are so obviously offended at having to do a job that you almost feel sorry for asking them for any assistance whatsoever.

Walking into your local theater, once a buzz with video game sounds, wild kids, coy couples on charming first dates, and an air of excitement, feels like trudging into some kind of Soviet-era Costco.

Half the time, as you await the trailers, you feel a sneaking suspicion that a voice might come from above intoning, 'Does anyone know how to run a projector?'

The stark reality is that many folks would rather watch the big screen in their living room than the bigger screen in their local multiplex.

But isn't this even more reason to bring back the majesty and power of the traditional movie palace to woo us with nostalgic awe and grandeur?

Americans need the movies. It is part of our heritage, our tradition. It was once an affordable middle-class luxury worth every penny. Now, it feels like paying for an insult.

To truly recover from the Covid lockdowns we need to have nice things again. We need bustling restaurant districts, full office buildings, clean and safe subways, and yes, we need the movie theaters to be an oasis, not a reminder of all we have lost.

To paraphrase a one-time blockbuster, if you slap on a paint job, mop the floor, and treat customers well, they 'will come'.

On the other hand, if we're only offered filth, an unfriendly face, and theaters with all the charm of a storage space center, then this industry, once a diamond, will fade to dust.

And if movie theaters go the way of the video stores, something of great value will be lost. Our kids will never know the magic and wonder that we all took for granted.

Let's not allow that to happen.

Now get off my lawn!
