A Doll's House (1879) is one of the revolutionary classics. The play has been staged throughout the world, and still is - one and a half-century later. It portrays the tragic hypocrisy of Victorian middle-class marriage on the stage.

A Doll's House is a three-act play written by Henrik Ibsen and directed by Kelly Roush. It was first performed at the Royal Theatre, Copenhagen, in Denmark. The play premiered on December 21, 1879, and is set in a Norwegian town circa. Its major subject is the awakening of a middle-class wife and mother.

A Doll's House short summary

The play's setting is in the home of Helmer and Nora Torvald in a Norwegian town in 1879. The play was written at a time when women were voiceless in society, and Nora breaks this norm by asking for a divorce from her husband. She courageously faces her husband and tells him that they need to sit down and talk things out when she feels her marriage is not working.

Act one

The play takes place on Christmas Eve, and Torvald has just received a promotion to work in the bank. His wife, Nora, is excited about his new position, for she feels it will relieve their money problems. The couple has three young children who are being cared for by the nanny, Kristine Linde. Nora's old friend arrives at their house, having lost her husband and mother.

Nora gets the idea that her husband can offer her friend a job, for she is jobless. She tells her friend how she had forged her father's signature to take a loan to treat her husband, and she had never told anyone about it, for women were forbidden from taking loans.

A Doll's House themes

What is the main theme of A Doll's House? There are several themes in the play. Here is a list of the primary themes:

Gender bias

Gender bias is the main theme of the play. The voice of the woman has been suppressed by society when it comes to marriage, motherhood, and decision making. Nora is not allowed to make any major decisions, even those affecting her own happiness.

Torvald believes that a woman is supposed to be a good wife and a mother, and he tells Nora that women are responsible for the morality of their children. Nora stays in a society where women are not allowed to take loans, and she is forced to forge her dying father's signature to get a loan to facilitate her husband's trip to Italy for treatment.

Love and marriage

Nora and Torvald are presented as a married couple. They use pet names to refer to each other, which shows the love that exists between them. At the end of the play, we see Mrs. Linde and Krogstad get married. Dr. Rank has never married, and he confesses the love he has always had for Nora, even though Nora is a married woman.

Deception

Another important theme in the play is deception. Nora forges her dying father's signature in order to get a loan. Krogstad helps her do all these and then, later on, uses it to blackmail her. Nora never discloses this to her husband because the society does not allow women to borrow loans.

Materialism

Materialism is another major theme running through the play. At the beginning of the play, we see Nora admiring her sitting room, which she sees as perfectly furnished. Torvald's perception of being rich is by how much money one has.

He refuses to take on cases that do not satisfy him, which is the reason for his success. Krogstad later tells Nora that he even wants a top position at the bank.

Money and work

The need for money is a recurring theme in the play. At the beginning of the play, Torvald is promoted, and he earns a "big fat income". However, this does not deter him from chastising Nora for spending too much. He argues that they need to exercise caution when it comes to how they spend their finances.

You also see Nora being proud of the fact she raised money for her and Torvald to make a trip to Italy all by herself. However, she owes a debt that becomes a source of dread, shame, and terror. The need for money is also reflected in Mrs. Linde's story. She is in desperate need of a job following the demise of her husband.

Individual vs. society

Nora and Mrs. Linde find a sense of meaning in their lives through serving others and performing the caring, obedient role that society requires of them. Throughout the play, Nora puts the needs of others before herself. She thinks little of how her act of forgery and debt to Krogstad affect her personally.

Instead, she is worried about how they might impact the lives of her husband and children. At the end of the play, she chooses to put herself as an individual instead of what society expects of her. Mrs. Linde admits that, without a husband or any family members to care for, she feels that her life is pointless.

Reputation

In the play, Nora and Dr. Rank's reputation hinges on that of their parents. The bad reputation of their parents comes back to haunt them, regardless of any goodwill they have build up on their own. In the case of Dr. Rank, his father had a reputation of enjoying physical pleasures to a damaging extent.

Even though he is an upstanding member of society, he is punished for his father's misdeeds. In the case of Nora, Torvald tells her that she inherited her father's reputation as a careless spender.

Sacrificial role of women in the society

The female characters in the play sacrifice their integrity to support their families. Mrs Linde is forced to abandon Krogstad even though she is her true love. She gets married to a richer man to support her mother and two brothers. The nanny in the story is forced to abandon her own child to work as the caretaker of Nora's children to support herself.

What is the irony in A Doll's House?

There are many instances of irony in the play. In the first act, Torvald tells his wife that they should not borrow money because it is a shameful act as there will be no freedom and beauty in their home. He does not know that the wife already has an impending loan.

Torvald decides to fire Krogstad, for he feels physically ill when in the company of such people who forge documents. It is ironic because he stays with his wife, who also forged a signature.

Nora tells Mrs. Linde that her husband is so much in love with her. This turns out to be false as she comes to learn that he is a selfish and egocentric man. He appears to be morally upright but never proves his moral uprightness. He even tells Nora that she inherited her father's bad conduct.

Which three themes are present in Ibsen's A Doll's House?

The three primary themes in A Doll's House are gender bias, love and marriage, and deception. Because of gender bias, the voice of the woman has been suppressed by society when it comes to marriage, motherhood, and decision making.

The marriage theme is reflected in the life of Nora and Torvald and that Mrs. Linde and Krogstad are presented as married couples. Deception is not only shown by the main characters but even the supporting characters.
