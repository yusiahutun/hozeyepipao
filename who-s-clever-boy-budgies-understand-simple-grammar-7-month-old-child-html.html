Budgies are the first species other than humans that have been seen to learn grammar

March 16, 2024

Budgerigars, as members of the parrot family, have long been known to be able to mimic human speech.

But now researchers have found that their brains are also able to pick out abstract patterns in language and phrases.

This makes the birds the first non-human species to be able to grasp simple grammar, according to a new study.

But now researchers have found that their brains are also able to pick out abstract patterns in language. This makes the birds the first non-human species to be able to grasp simple grammar, according to a new study

A FIRST FOR NON-HUMANS 

The researchers from the University of Leiden said that the ability to pick out the abstract pattern is ‘something long thought to be unique for humans and known from only a few species.’

They said that while similar tests have been carried out on a variety of other animals, such as rats, rhesus monkeys, and birds have ‘not provided unambiguous evidence that they can do so’. 

‘Nonhuman species ability for such abstraction has been debated, and until now, had not been clearly shown,’ the authors write in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

While the birds' ability is only about that of a seven-month old child, the researchers say this is ‘still pretty impressive’ – and gives budgies a similar ability to that of a human baby at seven months old – who are also able to pick out the abstract pattern in a series of meaningless words.

For example, a baby is able to realise that ‘ga-ti-ga’ and ‘wo-fi-wo’ have a similar pattern which can be described as XYX where the first ‘syllable’ is repeated after a different syllable.

Scientists at the University of Leiden and Duke University in the US put six budgerigars through their paces.

They played them five bursts of song made of three parts, with the structure X-X-Y, or X-Y-X.

Each time they heard XXY they got a reward if they pecked at a lever.

The bursts of song were all different, so the only way the birds would be able to know how to get a reward would be to pick out the pattern.

But if they got it wrong, the light in their cage went out for a few seconds – which the birds did not enjoy.

The researchers from the University of Leiden said that the ability to pick out the abstract pattern is ‘something long thought to be unique for humans and known from only a few species.’

‘Nonhuman species ability for such abstraction has been debated, and until now, had not been clearly shown,’ the authors write in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.

Michelle Spiering, lead author of the paper said: ‘Learning grammar is one of the most important abilities for learning a language.’

She said that the reason budgies, which are members of the parrot family, are cleverer than other birds may be because they ‘sing more flexible songs with more different sounds’. 

Unlike many other birds species, budgies and others in the parrot family learn new songs all their lives, while most songbirds learn their songs during a brief phase in their youth.

She added budgies are also highly sociable, living in large groups ‘and these abilities are relevant to learning songs and recognising other individuals in the group.’
