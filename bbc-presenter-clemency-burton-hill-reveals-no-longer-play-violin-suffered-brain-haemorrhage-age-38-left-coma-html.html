BBC presenter Clemency Burton-Hill reveals she can no longer play the violin after she suffered a br

February 15, 2024
blog

'I tried to speak – the words were clear in my brain but came out as gobbledygook. I was terrified. Then suddenly the world went dark.'

That is the terrifying recollection of Clemency Burton-Hill MBE author and classical music podcaster who suffered a massive stroke and brain haemorrhage four years ago in January 2020.

Speaking today on Women's Hour, Londoner Clemency revealed some of the ways the situation - which left her undergoing emergency surgery and spending seven-hours in a coma - affected her.

While she was left unable to move her right side, and therefore lost the ability to play the violin, she still ensured creativity came from the disaster.

Clemency has just published the latest book in her bestselling year of wonder series, called Journal of Wonder.

Clemency Burton-Hill, MBE, who is an author and classical music podcaster presenting BBC Radio 3

The tome takes you from January to December with classical music suggestions for every day. 

Clemency, who now lives in the US, is back in the UK - a familiar feeling as a Londoner, born and bred.

While she said she is enjoying being home, she added: 'I like to think that I'm a very international human being.

'I don't think of myself as a British person or English person...I've always felt like a Londoner, and I think I wasn't letting myself really miss it, because it I couldn't be here and also, there was so much going on.'

Of course, just weeks after she suffered from the stroke, the world went into lockdown with the pandemic, throwing her world even further into chaos. 

Because of how the stroke affected her ability to play music, Clemency felt a strong sense of frustration about how 'music is really for everyone'. 

She explained: 'It's a common good in a way. [All music] shouldn't have boundaries or barriers to entry, but particularly classical music [shouldn't].'

Clemency continued: 'It often makes people feel shut out. And I felt I was incredibly privileged, that I could be able to be in that space and also on the other side. 

LONDONER: Clemency is a Londoner born and bred, but is currently living in the US, and says she thinks of herself as 'a very international human being'

'I've been lucky to have music in my whole life. All through my life, but also many other interests in very many different pursuits. And I always felt this is so wrong, that there's a kind of fake barrier like if...you don't know the lingo, or you don't know what something means, you shouldn't be in the space of classical music and that I found incredibly distressing and also really, absolutely not true.'

It was largely this sense of distress that led to Clemency wanting to make music accessible to as many people as possible, via her series of books, which makes recommendations for people, helping them navigate the often overwhelming world of classical music.

Speaking about how listening to music has helped her, she explained: 'In the beginning, after my brain ruptured, it was so discombobulating - obviously everything was - but not being able to feel or hear music as I used to do [especially].

'And obviously I can't use my right side really at all. So unfortunately, I'm not able to play the violin again. I can't do many, many of the things that I really relied on in my former life. '

She is the author of the Year of Wonder series, which introduces people to listen to different music every day

But the music, the actual listening to it, just feels completely now, back in terms of how I felt, how safe I feel in it. So that's a wonderful thing.' 

Clemency Burton-Hill has written before about how badly she was affected by the stroke, and how lucky she feels to have survived the way she has.

Writing in 2020, she said: 'I'm aware that I’ve been so, so lucky. The AVM is still there – Dr Kellner and his team are treating it with radiotherapy – and there is a long road ahead. 

'But it is a salutary reminder to live every day to its fullest. Life is a miracle. I am so grateful that, because of the exceptional medical care I was privileged to have access to, I survived. '

And now, she has used that perspective to help others appreciate classical music, through her award-winning writing.
