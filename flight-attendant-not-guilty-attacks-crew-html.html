AA flight attendant slapped crew and tried to open the door found not guilty due to insanity

January 30, 2024
blog

Former flight attendant Joanne Snow, above, was found not guilty by reason of insanity

An American Airlines flight attendant accused last year of attacking crew members and air marshals during a trans-Atlantic flight has been found not guilty by reason of insanity.

Joanne Snow, 67, had faced charges of interference with flight crews and attendants, and with assaulting, resisting or impeding an officer or employee of the United States. She faced 25 years in prison.

Snow, who had worked for the airline for 45 years, had attacked co-workers and two US air marshals and tried to open the jet’s door as it taxied for takeoff, according to the Charlotte Observer.

This week, federal prosecutors reduced the charges to simple assault. 

Snow, above, became extremely agitated on a round trip flight in November 2015

Lawyers for both sides agreed that Snow had reacted badly to some prescription medication and was no longer a danger to herself or others 

In a Charlotte courtroom on Friday, both sides stipulated that the flight attendant had reacted badly to some prescription drugs taken before the flight, and had been treated by a psychiatrist and was no longer a threat to the public.

US Magistrate Judge David Cayer agreed, and accepted the not-guilty plea recommended by attorneys.

'She is not a criminal. If she had been removed and given the psychiatric treatment she needed, none of this would have happened,' said coworker Cyndy Atkins in January.

On November 23, 2015, US Air Marshal Joseph Fialka said in an affidavit that while American Airlines Flight 704 was en route to Frankfurt, Germany, from Charlotte, one flight attendant told him that Snow slapped her in the face while they were in the aircraft's middle galley.

American Airlines was criticized for not properly handling the matter when flight attendant Joanne Snow began acting erratically on a flight in November 2015

Fialka said Snow later shoved him several times on his shoulder and arm as he was seated, then went to the back of the plane and grabbed the collar of another air marshal.

After those incidents, Fialka said he was talking with another air marshal when Snow became upset and hit him in the chest with an open palm.

Fialka said Snow and the same crew were assigned to the return flight from Frankfurt to Charlotte on November 25. When the plane arrived in Charlotte, Fialka said Snow tried to get past a passport control officer.
